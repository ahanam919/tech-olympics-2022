Sharps

What are Sharps?

The term “sharps” refers to, well, anything sharp. People with ailments such as diabetes and allergies often use these on a daily basis. Sharps commonly include:

  • Needles – hollow needles used to inject drugs (medication) under the skin
  • Syringes – devices used to inject medication into or withdraw fluid from the body
  • Lancets, also called “fingerstick” devices – instruments with a short, two-edged blade used to get drops of blood for testing. Lancets are commonly used in the treatment of diabetes.
  • Auto Injectors, including epinephrine and insulin pens – syringes prefilled with fluid medication designed to be self-injected into the body
  • Infusion sets – tubing systems with a needle used to deliver drugs to the body.
  • Connection needles/sets – needles that connect to a tube used to transfer fluids in and out of the body. This is generally used for patients on home hemodialysis.

How to Get Your Sharps Ready for Disposal:

  • Making sure that you pick it up from the NON-NEEDLE part of the item
  • Using gloves is highly recommended, especially if you think any medicine/blood could be lingering on a needle tip.
  • If you have access to obtaining an FDA approved sharps disposal box, put all the sharps in it, stopping if the container reaches ¾ capacity. If you stop before that, that’s fine, but if you have leftover instruments, using another bottle is required.
  • If you don’t have access to the sharps box, don’t fret! The FDA allows a household bottle, such as a hard plastic container (Ex. laundry detergent). Follow the same steps as listed above.
  • Using the pharmacy finder below, enter your zip code and look at the map, locating the pharmacy that provides the easiest access for you.
  • Then, secure the lid of your container with tape for good measure, and make your way to the pharmacy/collection center!

Make Sure NOT To:

  • Touch the tip of the needle with your bare hands
  • Enter the collection center/pharmacy with a pile of needles and medical instruments; make sure to always use a hard plastic container.
  • Use a home container that:
  • Doesn’t have a secure airtight lid
  • Isn’t made of hard plastic (Ex. plastic water bottles are very flimsy plastic, and can be easily punctured.)
  • Don’t interact with needles used by other people. To summarize, cross contamination can apply to everything.
  • Don’t try to flush, throw away, or recycle your sharps. They aren’t recyclable. Trust me.
