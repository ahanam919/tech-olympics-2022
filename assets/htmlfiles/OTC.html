Over the Counter Medications (OTCs)

What are OTCs?

OTC’s, or Over the Counter medications, is a label that we give medications/ointments that help to heal the body, but are not necessarily strong enough to require a doctor’s approval. The “drugs” found in these medications won’t provide absolutely lethal overdose, like some prescribed medicines very easily can.

Common examples include Ibuprofen, Tylenol, Midol, Claritin, etc.

What’s the difference between OTCs and Prescribed Medications?

  1. OTCs have a “wider margin” of safety, meaning most people can use them without ill side effects, as opposed to more specifically tailored prescriptions.
  2. OTCs are often administered in lower doses.
  3. OTC drugs’ only purpose should be for minor ailments.
  4. Generalizing, OTC’s are often more cheap, considering that they aren’t personalized or require many strong ingredients.

The way to know if a certain medication bottle in the back of your “overdue for a cleaning” medicine cupboard is OTC:

  • PAY ATTENTION TO THE LABELS!!!: This is the key resource to determining how to dispose of those old bottles.
  • Let’s break it down:
  • OTC Labels:
  • Are short and sweet
  • Often have six main parts (sometimes a seventh for the company’s phone number if the consumer has questions):
  • Active Ingredient and Purpose.
  • Uses.
  • Warnings.
  • Directions.
  • Other Information.
  • Inactive Ingredients.
  • *Questions or Comments.*
  • The packaging and shape of these bottles is often very varied, contrasting with Prescribed medicines, which we will go over soon.
