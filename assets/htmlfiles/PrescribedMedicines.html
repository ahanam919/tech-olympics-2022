Prescribed Medicines (PMs)

What are Prescribed Medications?

Prescribed Medications are prescription drugs that require a doctor/medical expert’s approval to be acquired. They are much stronger than OTCs, and are used for more severe ailments and injuries. The disposal and use of these drugs should be taken more seriously, since their exposure to the wrong people could result in disease and death.

What’s the difference between OTCs and Prescribed Medications?

  1. Prescribed Medications have a very small margin of safety. This means that they are tailored to the person, and can't be used by the general population without causing adverse side effects in a majority of people.
  2. PM’s are often administered at higher/more frequent doses.
  3.  Prescribed drugs’ only purpose should be for bad ailments. Leftover PM’s should NOT EVER be used for a minor ailment. (ex. Don’t use a PM that you got for a severe fever again when you get a minor cold. There’s a reason they don’t administer that medication unless things are significantly bad.)
  4. Generalizing, Prescribed medications are often more expensive, considering the personalization that goes into them.

The way to know if a certain medication bottle in the back of your “overdue for a cleaning” medicine cupboard are Prescribed Medicines:

  • PAY ATTENTION TO THE LABELS!!!: This is the key resource to determining how to dispose of those old bottles.
  • Let’s break it down:
  • Prescribed Medicine Labels:
  • One of the most tell-tale signs of prescription is often what the bottle looks like: a bright orange cylinder with a white label, printed with the type of medicine and likely yours or a family member’s name on it.  
  • Much more complex
  • Depending on how you stored the medicine, there might be a pharmacy information sheet that came with it.
  • If you didn’t keep that paper, don’t worry! It’s very easy to distinguish between OTC and Prescribed Medicines simply by looking to see if the bottle DOESN’T have the OTC Drug Facts.
  • Sometimes, the bottle will have additional instruction stickers slapped on, like “only for ORAL use” (maybe someone tried to stick it up their nose?), or “Take with food.”
  • All jokes aside, do take these labels seriously. They exist to protect you!
